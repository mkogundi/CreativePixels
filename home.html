    Vauxhall, more deep into history than you expect!

    Vauxhall, more deep into history than you expect!

    If you think winter in London 2015 is going to be dreary and gloomy, you are probably right. But if you think sun cannot shine during winter, you would definitely be in larger group of people who got it wrong. December 298h is most unlikely day one can expect in London during peak cold days of winter, loads of sun and gentle breeze and you start wondering if you are still in London.

    Out of all possible options to spend such day, I chose the Vauxhall bridge in the south bank area, definitely one of cleanest and peaceful stretches along Thames. The placard placed along the walk says, it’s been worshipped, feared, respected, worked, abused, traversed, ignored, restored and enjoyed. Am glad I am doing the last bit of the declaration, enjoying the lovely day! But for those curious minds about why this place was/is worshipped, feared and respected, please feel free to sit along with me on this bench on the river bank and enjoy the history that would unfold before you.

    On the contrary to the popular belief that area derives its name from Guy Fawkes (who planned to assassinate King James I by blowing up the House of Lords in 1605), there is no connection with the him and is more of a misconception. The actual story would start from the early 13th century, when Anglo-Norman mercenary named Falkes de Breauté built a manor house in the then empty marshlands of South Lambeth, across the River Thames from Westminster. In 1223–24, de Breauté and others revolted against Henry III; following a failed attempt to seize the Tower of London, de Breauté's lands in England were forfeited and he was forced into exile in France and later Rome. The lands surrounding his Lambeth manor house continued to be known as Falkes' Hall, later Vauxhall. Breauté also had his own coat of arms and chose Griffin, a symbol now synonymous with Vauxhall.

    Thames being pretty wide at this point, had quite a few small islands and marshy geography. Though often being a flooded area, it certainly lead to better soil fertility and hence farming. This was the era of worshipping, when residents offered spears and food into water as offering to gods. During the dark ages, it garnered ill repute and fame for mysterious paths within the marshy shores, as the time moved on people discovered the abundant wildfowl and the area thrived on hunting and then transport across the Thames. The two river tributaries, Effra (from south) and Tyburn (from north) also join in at this place, though they can be witnessed only in extremely low tide time due to the bricked banks.

    The fame and population lead to the obvious, cutting down of the woodland and construction of man-made banks, resulting in declining health of the local wildlife. Men started to meddle with the nature, reshaped the river as per the needs, assumed that Effra was no longer useful, and finally blocked it up. In 1700, the shores were dotted with windmills and were famous for local potteries which with time, slowly changed to vinegar and gas industries. The industrial era, aided by poor housing and sanitation for the growing population and industries, ensured that the Vauxhall became centre of Cholera epidemic. Combined with sewer waste, the river water became so thick with pollution, that in 1858 ‘The Great Stink’ caused parliament to close!

    The previous known name for the river crossing was the Regent bridge, built between 1809 and 1816 and was built on the site of a former ferry called Huntley Ferry. As part of the development plans for the south bank, the Vauxhall Gardens pleasure park was opened, it was also used as the launch point for hot air balloon flights. Large crowds would gather on the bridge and surrounding streets to watch the flights. One of the most interesting view I believe would have been in September 1844 when Mister Barry, a clown from Astley's Amphitheatre, set the sail from Vauxhall Bridge to Westminster Bridge in a washtub towed by geese! In the 1990s sightseeing balloon flights from Vauxhall Gardens – by then renamed back to Spring Gardens – were resumed. The service closed in 2001 following the opening of the London Eye.

    Coming back to our yesteryear Vauxhall journey, by now the river was awash with industrial waste and raw sewage during the industrial period. This together with loss of riverside habitat, insured that river was considered biologically dead by 20th century. But then as the industrial period aged out and water was cleaned up, the banks river started regaining colours. The area now changed the roles from the cold storage units to major crossing across the river, slowly the fish population started to re-establish its presence and by 1974 salmon had returned to Thames.

    The current Vauxhall Bridge is a Grade II listed (means it is prevented from any modern day changes, helping it to retain its character) steel and granite deck arch bridge connecting the Vauxhall on the south bank and Pimlico on the north bank. Opened in 1906, the design and appearance of the current bridge has remained almost unchanged since. The 8 statues along the bridges were created by Drury and Pomeroy. On the upstream piers are Pomeroy's Agriculture, Architecture (holding a model of St Paul's Cathedral, smallest cathedral in London), Engineering and Pottery, whilst on the downstream piers are Drury's Science, Fine Arts, Local Government and Education. Each statue weighs approximately two tons! This second bridge, which took eight years to build, was the first in London to carry trams and later one of the first two roads in London to have a bus lane.

    If you happen to be lucky enough to have visited the place in extreme low tides, a remnant of the earliest known bridge-like structure in London can be seen alongside Vauxhall Bridge. Dating to between 1550 BC and 300 BC, it consists of two rows of wooden posts, which it is believed would originally have carried a deck of some kind. It is believed that it did not cross the whole river, but instead connected the south bank to an island, possibly used for burial of the dead. As no mention of this or similar structures in the area is made in Julius Caesar's account of crossing the Thames nor by any other Roman author, it is presumed that the structure had been dismantled or destroyed prior to Caesar's expedition to Britain in 55 BC.

    Did you know that during the Second World War, worried that the bridge might be targeted to cut off the transportation, a temporary bridge known as Millbank Bridge was built parallel to Vauxhall Bridge, 200 yards downstream for transporting the tanks and other heavy military equipment. Eventually the Vauxhall Bridge survived the war undamaged, and in 1948 Millbank Bridge was dismantled. Its girders were shipped to Northern Rhodesia and used to span a tributary of the Zambezi.

    Today this place is part of the thriving riverside community, recipients of Queens Award for sustainable development and home to over 120 species of fish! Sitting here on holiday morning with bright blue sky, watching an amphibious bus entering Thames via a slipway on the south bank, reliving the history indeed made my day! Hope you too enjoyed it and had a great time! Images to soon come on the web page, keep watching!
